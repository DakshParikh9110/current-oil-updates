Global impact — who feels this most?

Critical risk

Japan

90% exposed

Near-total Hormuz dependency. No domestic shale. Facing the sharpest import-driven inflation of any G7 nation.

Critical risk

South Korea

70% exposed

Heavy Gulf dependency. A major petrochemical exporter — feedstock disruption squeezes both imports and export income.

High risk

India

55% exposed

Caught between Hormuz dependency and discounted Russian barrels. Russian imports provide a partial buffer — but can't fully offset the shock.

Partially buffered

China

40% exposed

Large strategic reserves + Russian pipeline imports cushion the blow. But naphtha and feedstock gaps are already being reported across industry.

Moderate risk

Europe

~20% exposed

Lower direct Hormuz exposure. Energy transition softens the blow — renewables cover ~40% of EU power. Industry costs still climb.

Largely insulated

USA

<5% exposed

Domestic shale at ~13 mb/day + SPR releases absorb the shock. Gasoline near $4.30/gal — but far less exposed than Asian peers.

Global

Inflation

+0.5–1% CPI

Oil is a cost input for ~70% of manufactured goods. Sustained $100+ Brent threatens the rate cuts markets have priced in for 2026.

Global

Petrochemicals

Disrupted

Naphtha and feedstock shortages forcing output curbs across Asia. Plastics, fertilizers, synthetics — all downstream industries affected.

The big picture: Today's 15% crash is markets pricing a ceasefire — but the Strait isn't open yet. If talks collapse, $120+ Brent returns fast. If it reopens fully, prices drift toward $80–90 on soft underlying fundamentals. JPMorgan's pre-conflict base case was $60/bbl. That $60 spread between peace and war is the widest geopolitical risk premium in modern oil market history — and it hasn't resolved yet.
